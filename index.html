Statistic Conditions Distribution Use Case

T-Statistic Population variance is unknown and estimated from the sample, typically used for smaller sample sizes (n < 30). T-distribution with degrees of freedom related to sample size. Used for hypothesis testing when population variance is unknown, especially with smaller sample sizes.

Understanding Null Hypothesis Significance Testing

Null Hypothesis Significance Testing (NHST) is a fundamental concept in statistics that helps in decision-making processes. It is a method used to determine if there is enough evidence in a sample of data to infer that a certain condition is true for the entire population.

What is the Null Hypothesis?

The null hypothesis \(H_{0}\) is a general statement or default position that there is no relationship between two measured phenomena or no association among groups. For example, \(H_{0}:\mu=0\).

What is the Alternative Hypothesis?

The alternative hypothesis \(H_{1}\) or \(H_{a}\) is what you might believe to be true or hope to prove true. For instance, \(H_{1}:\mu\neq0\) in a two-tailed test.

Significance Level \(\alpha\)

The significance level \(\alpha\) is a threshold set by the researcher which defines the probability of rejecting the null hypothesis when it is actually true. A common choice for \(\alpha\) is 0.05.

Decision Rule

If the p-value is less than or equal to the significance level, we reject the null hypothesis. This is considered statistically significant. Conversely, if the p-value is greater than \(\alpha\), we fail to reject the null hypothesis.

Step Description
Formulation of Hypotheses The null hypothesis (H0) typically states no effect or difference. The alternative hypothesis (H1) represents the effect or difference expected.
Selection of Significance Level (α) The threshold probability for rejecting H0. Commonly set at 0.05.
Test Statistic Calculation A measure derived from sample data, indicating the compatibility of the data with H0.
Calculation of P-value The probability of obtaining the observed data, or more extreme, under H0.
Decision Rule The pre-determined rule stating that if the p-value ≤ α, H0 is rejected.
Conclusion The final decision about H0 based on the comparison of the p-value and the significance level.

Introduction to p values

Aspect Detail Explanation
Definition p-value The p-value is the probability of obtaining test results at least as extreme as the observed results, under the assumption that the null hypothesis is correct.
Interpretation Low p-value A low p-value (typically ≤ 0.05) indicates strong evidence against the null hypothesis, so it is rejected.
Interpretation High p-value A high p-value indicates weak evidence against the null hypothesis, so it is not rejected.
Misconceptions p-value & hypothesis truth The p-value does not measure the probability that the null hypothesis is true or false. It only measures the evidence against the null hypothesis.
Significance Level (\( \alpha \)) Threshold The significance level is the threshold below which the p-value is considered strong enough evidence to reject the null hypothesis.
Context Dependence Importance of context The p-value should be considered in the context of the study, including the size of the effect and the practical significance, not just statistical significance.
Not an Error Probability Common misunderstanding The p-value is not the probability of making a mistake by rejecting a true null hypothesis (Type I error).

Estimating a test statistic

Step Action Description
1 State the Hypotheses Formulate the null hypothesis (H0) and the alternative hypothesis (H1). The null hypothesis typically represents the status quo or no effect.
2 Choose the Significance Level Select the significance level (\( \alpha \)), which is the probability of rejecting the null hypothesis when it is true. Common choices are 0.05 or 0.01.
3 Select the Appropriate Test Choose a statistical test based on the study design, data distribution, and whether the population variance is known. Common tests include Z-test, t-test, chi-squared test, etc.
4 Collect Data Gather the sample data which will be used to calculate the test statistic.
5 Calculate the Test Statistic Use the sample data to calculate the test statistic (e.g., Z-score or t-score) which measures how far the sample statistic deviates from the null hypothesis.
6 Find the P-value Determine the p-value which is the probability of observing the test statistic or one more extreme under the assumption that the null hypothesis is true.
7 Make a Decision Compare the p-value to the significance level. If the p-value is less than or equal to \( \alpha \), reject the null hypothesis. Otherwise, fail to reject it.
8 Report the Results Clearly present the findings of the hypothesis test, including the test statistic, p-value, and conclusion regarding the null hypothesis.

Overview

A test statistic is a standardized value that is calculated from sample data during a hypothesis test. It's used to determine whether to reject the null hypothesis.

Z-Test Statistic

The Z-test statistic is used when testing hypotheses about the population mean when the population standard deviation is known and the sample size is large.

The formula for the Z-test statistic is:

\[ Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}} \]

  • \( \bar{X} \) is the sample mean.
  • \( \mu_0 \) is the hypothesized population mean under the null hypothesis.
  • \( \sigma \) is the population standard deviation.
  • \( n \) is the sample size.

T-Test Statistic

The t-test statistic is used when the population standard deviation is unknown and the sample size is small.

The formula for the t-test statistic is:

\[ t = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}} \]

  • \( \bar{X} \) is the sample mean.
  • \( \mu_0 \) is the hypothesized population mean under the null hypothesis.
  • \( s \) is the sample standard deviation.
  • \( n \) is the sample size.

Interpreting the Test Statistic

The calculated test statistic is compared against a critical value from a statistical distribution (such as the standard normal distribution for a Z-test or the t-distribution for a t-test) to determine whether to reject the null hypothesis.

Z-Test Statistic Calculation Example

Given the following information:

  • Population mean (\( \mu \)): 75
  • Sample mean (\( \bar{X} \)): 78
  • Population standard deviation (\( \sigma \)): 5
  • Sample size (\( n \)): 30

We can state our hypotheses as:

  • Null hypothesis (\( H_0 \)): \( \mu = 75 \)
  • Alternative hypothesis (\( H_1 \)): \( \mu \neq 75 \)

The Z-test statistic is calculated using the formula:

\[ Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}} \]

Plugging in the values we get:

\[ Z = \frac{78 - 75}{\frac{5}{\sqrt{30}}} \]

Calculating the Z-value gives us:

\[ Z \approx 2.915 \]

Now, we can use the Z-value to find the p-value and make a decision about our null hypothesis.

T Statistic Example

Scenario: Testing the effect of a new teaching method on student test scores.

  • Sample size (n): 25
  • Sample mean (\( \bar{X} \)): 82
  • Sample standard deviation (s): 6
  • Hypothesized population mean (\( \mu_0 \)): 80

Calculation

The t-test statistic is calculated as:

\[ t = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}} \]

Plugging in the values, we get:

\[ t = \frac{82 - 80}{\frac{6}{\sqrt{25}}} = \frac{2}{1.2} = 1.67 \]

Now, we can use the t-statistic to find the p-value and make a decision regarding our null hypothesis.

Review of Confidence Interval

Step Description
1. Determine Sample Statistics Calculate the sample mean (\( \bar{x} \)) and the sample standard deviation (s) from your data.
2. Choose Confidence Level Select the confidence level (e.g., 90%, 95%, 99%) for the interval.
3. Find the Critical Value Use a Z-table (for large samples or known population standard deviation) or a t-table (for small samples) to find the critical value corresponding to your chosen confidence level.
4. Calculate Margin of Error
  • For Z, use \( \text{Margin of Error} = Z \times \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \).
  • For t, use \( \text{Margin of Error} = t \times \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \), where n is the sample size.
5. Compute Confidence Interval Add and subtract the margin of error from the sample mean to get the lower and upper bounds of the confidence interval: \( \text{CI} = \bar{x} \pm \text{Margin of Error} \).

Sample Problem

A researcher wants to estimate the average height of adult women in a certain population. She collects a random sample of 35 women and records their heights. The average height in the sample is 165 cm, with a standard deviation of 5 cm. Calculate the 95% confidence interval for the average height of adult women in this population.

Solution

  1. Determine Sample Statistics:

    Sample Mean (\( \bar{x} \)): 165 cm
    Sample Standard Deviation (s): 5 cm
    Sample Size (n): 35

  2. Choose Confidence Level:

    95%

  3. Find the Critical Value:

    For a 95% confidence level, the critical value (t) from the t-distribution table with 34 degrees of freedom (n-1) is approximately 1.96.

  4. Calculate Margin of Error:

    \( \text{Margin of Error} = t \times \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \) = 1.96 x \( \frac{5}{\sqrt{35}} \)

  5. Compute Confidence Interval:

    Lower Bound = \( \bar{x} - \text{Margin of Error} \)
    Upper Bound = \( \bar{x} + \text{Margin of Error} \)
    Thus, the 95% confidence interval is calculated.

Extended Sample Problem

Following the previous study on the average height of adult women in a certain population, the researcher now wants to test the hypothesis that the average height of adult women in this population is different from the national average of 160 cm. She uses the same sample of 35 women, where the average height was 165 cm with a standard deviation of 5 cm. Conduct a hypothesis test at the 5% significance level and calculate the p-value.

Solution

  1. State the Hypotheses:

    Null Hypothesis (\( H_0 \)): The average height is 160 cm (\( \mu = 160 \)).
    Alternative Hypothesis (\( H_1 \)): The average height is not 160 cm (\( \mu \neq 160 \)).

  2. Calculate the Test Statistic:

    Use the formula for the t-statistic: \( t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}} \) = \( \frac{165 - 160}{\frac{5}{\sqrt{35}}} \).

  3. Determine the Degrees of Freedom:

    Degrees of Freedom (df) = n - 1 = 35 - 1 = 34.

  4. Calculate the p-value:

    Using the calculated t-statistic and the degrees of freedom, find the p-value from the t-distribution table or a statistical software.

  5. Conclusion:

    If the p-value is less than 0.05 (5% significance level), reject the null hypothesis. Otherwise, fail to reject the null hypothesis. The conclusion will provide insight into whether the average height significantly differs from the national average.

Understanding what we calculated

Type 1 vs Type 2 Error

Type 1 Error Type 2 Error
Definition Definition
Rejecting the null hypothesis when it is actually true (False Positive). Failing to reject the null hypothesis when it is actually false (False Negative).
Occurrence Occurrence
Occurs when the p-value is less than the significance level, leading to the incorrect rejection of a true null hypothesis. Occurs when the p-value is greater than the significance level, leading to the incorrect acceptance of a false null hypothesis.
Consequences Consequences
Mistaken belief in an effect that is not actually present, potentially leading to unnecessary actions or further research based on a false premise. Missed opportunity to identify a real effect or difference, potentially leading to lack of action or recognition of an important finding.
Example Example
Concluding that a medication is effective when it is not. Concluding that a medication is not effective when it actually is.

Type 1 Error (False Positive)

Scenario: The true average height of adult women in the population is actually 160 cm, which means the null hypothesis (H0: μ = 160 cm) is true.

Type 1 Error: The researcher concludes that the average height is significantly different from 160 cm based on her sample, even though in reality, it's not. This error occurs if the p-value is mistakenly found to be less than the significance level (0.05), leading to the incorrect rejection of a true null hypothesis.

Consequence: The researcher incorrectly believes that the average height in the population is different from 160 cm, potentially leading to misinformed decisions or further research based on this incorrect assumption.

Type 2 Error (False Negative)

Scenario: The true average height of adult women in the population is different from 160 cm, which means the null hypothesis (H0: μ = 160 cm) is false.

Type 2 Error: The researcher concludes that there is no significant difference in the average height from 160 cm based on her sample, even though in reality, there is a difference. This error occurs if the p-value is mistakenly found to be greater than the significance level (0.05), leading to the incorrect failure to reject a false null hypothesis.

Consequence: The researcher fails to identify a real difference in average height, potentially missing out on important findings or implications related to the height of women in that population.

In a legal trial: Defendant is presumed innocent, and is found guilty only if there is sufficient evidence— i.e., beyond a reasonable doubt.

Example of Type 1 error (Jumping the Gun)

  • Finding an innocent person guilty in a legal trial.
  • Deciding that an ineffective drug is effective based on clinical trial results.

Example of Type 2 error (Missing the Boat)

  • Finding a guilty person not guilty in a legal trial.
  • Deciding that an effective drug is ineffective based on clinical trial results.

Effect Sizes

Effect size is a measure of the magnitude of the difference between two groups or the strength of an association between two variables. It is commonly used in statistical hypothesis testing, especially in meta-analyses where it can help determine the significance of a study.

Property Description
Definition A measure of the strength of the relationship between two variables or the magnitude of an effect in the population.
Interpretation Effect sizes can be small, medium, or large, which helps in understanding the practical significance of research findings.
Importance Provides more information than p-values alone, indicating not just whether an effect exists but also the size of the effect.
Context Dependence The interpretation of effect size is context-dependent and should be considered alongside other factors like sample size and study design.
